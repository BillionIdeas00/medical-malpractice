Frequently Asked Questions About Medical Malpractice

Below are answers to common questions about medical malpractice claims. For specific legal advice about your situation, always consult with a qualified attorney.

General Questions

Q: What exactly is medical malpractice?

A: Medical malpractice occurs when a healthcare provider fails to meet the accepted standard of care in treating a patient, and this failure causes injury or harm. Not every medical mistake is malpractice—the provider must have deviated from what a reasonably competent provider would have done under similar circumstances.

Q: How do I know if I have a valid medical malpractice case?

A: You may have a case if you can prove all four elements:

  1. A doctor-patient relationship existed (duty)
  2. The provider breached the standard of care
  3. The breach directly caused your injury
  4. You suffered significant damages as a result

Most medical malpractice attorneys offer free consultations to evaluate whether your case has merit.

Q: If my treatment didn't work, do I have a malpractice case?

A: Not necessarily. Medicine is not an exact science, and doctors cannot guarantee results. A poor outcome alone doesn't constitute malpractice. You must prove that the provider failed to meet the standard of care and that this failure caused your injury.

Q: Can I sue if a doctor didn't diagnose my condition?

A: Possibly, if the failure to diagnose constituted a breach of the standard of care and caused you harm. Misdiagnosis or delayed diagnosis cases require proving that a competent doctor would have made the correct diagnosis under the same circumstances.

Time Limits and Deadlines

Q: How long do I have to file a medical malpractice lawsuit?

A: Time limits (statutes of limitations) vary by state, typically ranging from 1 to 4 years. The clock may start on the date of the negligent act, the date you discovered the injury, or when treatment ended—this also varies by state. Many states also have absolute deadlines (statutes of repose) regardless of when you discovered the injury. Contact an attorney immediately to protect your rights.

Q: What if I didn't discover the injury until years later?

A: Many states have a "discovery rule" that starts the clock when you discovered or reasonably should have discovered the injury. However, some states also have statutes of repose that create absolute deadlines. Time limits for children and cases involving foreign objects may be different. Consult an attorney immediately to determine your specific deadline.

Q: Is there a deadline to file a wrongful death claim?

A: Yes, and wrongful death statutes are often shorter than general malpractice deadlines—sometimes only 1-2 years. The clock typically starts on the date of death. Contact an attorney immediately if your loved one died due to suspected medical negligence.

Legal Process and Costs

Q: How much does it cost to hire a medical malpractice attorney?

A: Most medical malpractice attorneys work on a contingency fee basis, meaning you pay nothing upfront. The attorney receives a percentage (typically 33-40%) of any settlement or verdict. If you don't win, you typically owe nothing for attorney fees. However, you may be responsible for case expenses depending on your fee agreement.

Q: What are "case expenses" and who pays for them?

A: Case expenses include costs like medical expert fees, court filing fees, deposition costs, medical record retrieval, and trial exhibits. These can total $50,000-$100,000+ in complex cases. Most attorneys advance these costs and are reimbursed from any recovery. Your fee agreement should specify what happens to expenses if you lose.

Q: How long does a medical malpractice case take?

A: Medical malpractice cases typically take 2-4 years from filing to resolution, though some take longer. Cases that go to trial generally take longer than those that settle. The complexity of your case, court schedules, and various legal procedures all affect timing.

Q: Will my case go to trial?

A: Most medical malpractice cases settle before trial, often during settlement negotiations or mediation. However, your attorney must be prepared to take the case to trial if a fair settlement cannot be reached. Insurance companies offer better settlements to attorneys who are willing and able to try cases.

Q: Can I represent myself in a medical malpractice case?

A: While legally possible, it's strongly inadvisable. Medical malpractice cases are among the most complex in civil law, requiring medical knowledge, expert witnesses, substantial financial resources, and specialized legal expertise. The vast majority of self-represented plaintiffs lose their cases.

Evidence and Documentation

Q: What evidence do I need for a medical malpractice case?

A: Key evidence includes:

  • Complete medical records from all providers
  • Documentation of all medical expenses
  • Proof of lost income
  • Timeline of events
  • Photographs of injuries
  • Witness statements
  • Expert witness testimony (obtained by your attorney)

Q: How do I get my medical records?

A: Under HIPAA, you have a right to your medical records. Submit a written request to the medical records department of each healthcare provider. There may be reasonable copying fees. Your attorney can also obtain records through the legal process.

Q: What if the hospital or doctor won't give me my records?

A: Providers are required by law to provide your records within a reasonable time (typically 30 days). If they refuse or delay unreasonably, file a complaint with your state's health department or contact an attorney who can compel production through legal channels.

Q: Should I confront my doctor about the mistake?

A: You can ask questions about your care, but avoid confrontational discussions. Don't accuse them of malpractice or threaten legal action. Anything you say can be used against you later. Consult with an attorney before having detailed conversations about potential malpractice.

Damages and Compensation

Q: How much is my medical malpractice case worth?

A: Case value depends on many factors:

  • Severity and permanence of injuries
  • Medical expenses (past and future)
  • Lost income and earning capacity
  • Pain and suffering
  • Impact on quality of life
  • State damage caps
  • Strength of evidence
  • Venue and jurisdiction

An experienced attorney can provide a realistic estimate after reviewing your case.

Q: What are damage caps and how do they affect my case?

A: Many states limit ("cap") damages in medical malpractice cases, particularly non-economic damages like pain and suffering. Caps typically range from $250,000 to $1 million, though some states have no caps. These limits can significantly reduce potential recovery even in cases of severe injury.

Q: Will I have to pay taxes on my settlement or award?

A: Generally, compensation for physical injuries (including medical expenses, pain and suffering related to physical injury) is not taxable. However, lost wages, punitive damages, and interest are typically taxable. Consult a tax professional about your specific situation.

Q: Can I still get compensation if I was partly at fault?

A: It depends on your state's law. Most states allow recovery under "comparative negligence" rules but reduce your award by your percentage of fault. A few states use "contributory negligence," which bars recovery if you were at all at fault. Your attorney can explain how your state's law applies.

Specific Situations

Q: Can I sue a hospital for malpractice?

A: Yes, hospitals can be liable for:

  • Their own negligence (unsafe conditions, inadequate policies)
  • Negligence of their employed staff
  • Failure to properly credential doctors
  • Negligent hiring or supervision

Hospitals may not be liable for independent contractor doctors, though this varies by state and circumstance.

Q: What if the malpractice happened at a VA hospital or military facility?

A: Claims against government healthcare facilities fall under the Federal Tort Claims Act, which has different rules:

  • You must file an administrative claim within 2 years
  • The claim goes through an administrative process first
  • Special federal procedures apply
  • No jury trial—a federal judge decides

Consult an attorney experienced in federal tort claims immediately.

Q: Can I sue if malpractice caused my loved one's death?

A: Yes, through a wrongful death claim. Typically, surviving spouses, children, or parents can file these claims. Wrongful death cases often have shorter time limits and specific rules about who can recover and what damages are available. Contact an attorney immediately.

Q: What if I signed a consent form? Can I still sue?

A: Generally yes. Consent forms do not waive your right to sue for malpractice—they only acknowledge you were informed about risks. If negligence occurred, consent forms don't protect providers. However, you typically can't sue for known, disclosed risks that occurred despite proper care.

Q: Can I sue for malpractice that happened to my child?

A: Yes, parents can bring claims on behalf of minor children. Children often have extended statutes of limitations—in many states, they have several years after turning 18 to file. Birth injury cases may have special rules. Consult an attorney to understand your child's specific deadlines.

Expert Witnesses

Q: Why do I need a medical expert for my case?

A: Medical malpractice cases almost always require expert testimony because:

  • Experts explain the standard of care to the jury
  • They testify that the provider breached that standard
  • They establish causation between the breach and your injury
  • They explain complex medical concepts in understandable terms

Without qualified experts, you cannot prove your case.

Q: Who finds and pays for expert witnesses?

A: Your attorney identifies and retains qualified experts. The attorney typically advances expert fees (often $5,000-$50,000+ per expert) and is reimbursed from any recovery. Expert costs are one of the main expenses in medical malpractice litigation.

Q: Can my treating doctor be my expert witness?

A: Sometimes, but it's complicated. Treating doctors can testify about your injuries and treatment needs, but they may be reluctant to testify against other providers. You typically need separate experts to establish standard of care and breach.

Insurance and Settlement

Q: Should I talk to the insurance company?

A: Do not give statements to the provider's insurance company without consulting an attorney. They may try to get you to say things that damage your case. Politely decline and refer them to your attorney once you have one.

Q: The insurance company offered me a settlement. Should I take it?

A: Do not accept or sign anything without consulting an attorney. Early settlement offers are often much less than the true value of your case. Once you accept, you give up your right to sue. Get a legal opinion on whether the offer is fair.

Q: What if my health insurance paid for treatment? Do I still get those damages?

A: Yes, but your health insurer may have a "lien" or right to be repaid from your recovery. This is called "subrogation." Your attorney will negotiate with lienholders to reduce what must be repaid. Medicare and Medicaid liens are mandatory and must be repaid in full.

Q: Can I sue my insurance company for malpractice?

A: Generally no—insurance companies don't provide medical care. However, you might have other claims like bad faith denial of coverage or breach of contract. Different laws apply to insurance disputes versus medical malpractice.

Case Selection

Q: Why won't attorneys take my case?

A: Attorneys may decline cases for several legitimate reasons:

  • Statute of limitations has expired
  • Damages are insufficient to justify litigation costs
  • Cannot prove the standard of care was breached
  • Cannot prove causation
  • Case is too expensive relative to potential recovery
  • Lack of qualified experts willing to support the claim
  • State laws or damage caps make the case economically unfeasible

If multiple experienced attorneys decline your case, it likely isn't viable legally or economically.

Q: What if I can't afford an attorney?

A: Medical malpractice attorneys work on contingency, so you don't need money upfront. If an attorney believes your case has merit, they'll take it on contingency. If no attorney will take your case on contingency, it likely means the case isn't economically viable or lacks legal merit.

Moving Forward

Q: What should I do first if I think I'm a victim of medical malpractice?

A: Take these steps immediately:

  1. Ensure you're receiving proper medical care for your injuries
  2. Request copies of all your medical records
  3. Document everything: symptoms, expenses, conversations
  4. Preserve all evidence: bills, photos, correspondence
  5. Consult with a medical malpractice attorney as soon as possible
  6. Do NOT sign any settlement releases
  7. Do NOT give statements to insurance companies

Q: How do I find a good medical malpractice attorney?

A: Look for attorneys who:

  • Specialize in medical malpractice (not general practice)
  • Have significant experience and a track record of success
  • Have the resources to fund expensive litigation
  • Have access to qualified medical experts
  • Are willing to take cases to trial
  • Are in good standing with the state bar

Most offer free consultations. Meet with 2-3 attorneys before deciding.

Q: What questions should I ask during a consultation?

A: Important questions include:

  • How much experience do you have with medical malpractice?
  • Have you handled cases like mine?
  • What is your success rate?
  • Do you think I have a viable case?
  • What are the strengths and weaknesses of my case?
  • How long will this take?
  • What are your fees and how are expenses handled?
  • Who will actually work on my case?
  • How will you communicate with me?

Still Have Questions?

This FAQ covers common questions, but every case is unique. For answers specific to your situation, consult with a qualified medical malpractice attorney in your state. Most offer free consultations and can provide personalized guidance.
